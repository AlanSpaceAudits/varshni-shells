Varshni 1976 — Quasar Redshift Shells

Visual demos accompanying Y. P. Varshni's paper "The Red Shift Hypothesis for Quasars: Is the Earth the Center of the Universe?", Astrophysics and Space Science 43 (1976) 3–8.

Varshni grouped 384 quasars into 57 sets whose emission-line redshifts agreed to within ±0.001. Under the cosmological interpretation of redshift, each group's members share a common distance from Earth and therefore lie on a spherical shell centred on Earth. The combined chance probability for all 57 coincidences was ≈ 3 × 10⁻⁹⁹.

Varshni considered three geometric explanations for the shells, and argued the first two were not supported by the data:

(1) coincidence in distances by clustering — ruled out by direction data;
(2) a crystal-lattice arrangement with Earth at a node — ruled out by absence of regular angular pattern;
(3) Earth is the centre of the universe — accepted as the only remaining possibility under the cosmological interpretation.

The demos let you inspect each possibility yourself.

Geometric note on the move-observer test

Each quasar has a fixed 3D position in the demo (computed from its catalog RA/Dec and redshift-distance). The set of distances from Earth to one group's members is uniform by construction (that is the whole point of the shell). When the observer translates by a vector O, the new distance to each quasar becomes √((P − O)·(P − O)), which depends on direction. A spread of distances — not a single value — emerges. The fit score quantifies that spread; it sits at 1.00 at Earth and falls toward 0 elsewhere.

This is purely Euclidean geometry. The same conclusion holds in flat-FRW comoving coordinates if the quasar redshifts are real cosmological distances.
